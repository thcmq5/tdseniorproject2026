From Skilled Tradition to Rapid Fabrication: The Evolution of Garment Production and its Impact on the Modern Day Fashion Scene

Although it has never been fully agreed upon exactly when clothing first emerged, most archaeological and anthropological research suggests that humans began producing garments in prehistoric times primarily for survival. Early clothing served practical purposes, including protection from cold climates, harsh terrain, and environmental exposure, rather than aesthetic or cultural expression. During the Paleolithic era, early humans relied heavily on animal hides and furs due to their durability and insulating properties, which were well suited to Ice Age conditions (Harvard). As human societies developed, so did their ability to manipulate natural materials. Over time, early populations began experimenting with plant fibers, gradually transitioning from purely hide-based garments to more complex textile structures.The earliest evidence of true weaved fabric dates back to approximately 30,000-36,000 years ago, found at Dzudzuana Cave, in what is now the country of Georgia. Archaeologists uncovered flax fibers that showed signs of twisting, weaving, and even dyeing, indicating an advanced understanding of fiber manipulation for that period (Harvard). While earlier tools potentially associated with clothing production, such as bone awls and needle fragments, have been found at sites like Sibudu Cave in South Africa, the Dzudzuana discovery remains the earliest clear example of intentional textile production (Hoffecker). After some later advancements, yarn was starting to be made on small hand spindles, out of plant fiber or animal wool. This yarn would then be placed on rudimentary looms, where it was weaved into a usable fabric. Bone sewing needles with drilled eyelets have been found dating back around 20,000 years ago, pointing to the fact that people in the Ice Age had the ability to sew hides, and fabrics together (Hoffecker). By the Bronze and Iron age, metal needles and even thimbles have been found. Weaving devices evolved from being simple wooden frames to being more full fledged looms that you might think of today. These were often horizontal ground looms, or two-beam upright looms. With these advancements, weavers were able to greatly increase production speeds, especially after the introduction to the foot treadle loom. These were most common in Ancient Egypt. Eventually, these inventions spread throughout the world, becoming a key part to lots of cultures and economies. In the Middle East, the Fertile Crescent civilizations domesticated flax for linen and raised sheep for wool. Ancient Egyptians were renowned for their fine linens, as the Pharaohs and other elites wore them as a status symbol. Their linen was also the material of choice when wrapping the bodies for mummification (Wegner). Further east, South Asia was an early center for cotton cultivation, as archaeological evidence shows that cotton was being grown and spun in the Indus region by around 5000 BCE. India was actually praised for their linens as well; ancient Greek and Roman writers praised the light, airy “Indian muslins.” The Romans imported vast quantities of cotton cloth from India (known as succi) as well as eventually luxurious Chinese silk. In East Asia, China began sericulture (silk production) by the Neolithic period, and going by legend, this dates back to roughly 2700 BCE. By the Shang and Zhou dynasties, the Chinese had crafted a masterful process of raising silkworms, and weaving silk fabric. This allowed them to share the process with neighboring countries, but even then, silk was a complicated and expensive process. It became a global luxury, leading to the opening of the famous Silk Road, in 114 BCE. Through this trade route, the secret of silk spread, and more and more people had access to it. On the other side of the ocean, the Indigenous people of North America were also spinning cotton by about 4000 BCE, and in Mesoamerica, the Mayas were doing it by 1000 BCE. By the classical era, textile production had become integral to economies and trade. Linen, wool, cotton and silk, were all key items moving through the trade pipeline, enriching the market, culture, and social hierarchy. By modern standards, ancient fashion production was a small-scale operation relying on entirely natural and renewable materials. Fibers like flax, cotton, or wool were all grown or raised locally to the production, and fabrics were all biodegradable. Because of textile labor intensive production, clothing was relatively highly priced, meaning most people owned fewer garments, often resulting in the clothing being cared for at a much higher level, leading to increased longevity of the garment. This meant that historically there has never been much waste in terms of clothing. Old clothes were patched or fully repurposed rather than discarded. Natural dyes were used to color fabrics (from plants, minerals, and insects), and while sometimes the process would cause some minor localized pollution, the overall environmental impact was limited by the low production volume. Fast forward to the medieval period, textile production had become integral to the cultural aspect of society rather than purely domestic necessity. In Europe, fabric was created through guild systems, where production could be properly organized and streamlined. Most weaving and garment construction was still done in small workshops by hand, creating a specialization and isolation of the trade. This specialization of labor marked an important shift towards organized production rather than individual practice (Boucher). Wool was the dominant fabric produced in Europe during this period, largely due to the high sheep population, along with wool's suitability for cooler climates. Linen, derived from flax, also stayed very relevant and in production, as it was used in lots of undergarments and household textiles (ThoughtCo). Although this was the period where clothing became more of a cultural staple, technological improvements were relatively slow. Small improvements were made to the loom design, which did however lay the framework for industrial improvements in the future. The horizontal ground looms and upright two beam looms became more refined, as their frames were widened, allowing for wider fabric to be produced with finer threads and more consistent weaving patterns. These improvements did increase production quality and efficiency, but there was not a notable spike in production sizes, as manual labor was still needed, and still limited (New World Encyclopedia). Clothing remained an expensive and limited craft, enforcing social hierarchies in which clothing represented class, wealth, and professionalism. By the late medieval and early renaissance periods, the trade network continued to expand, reshaping the economy of textiles. The introduction of cotton from south Asia, and silk from China, into the European markets brought lighter and more decorative fabrics to that side of the world, expanding consumer demand for higher end clothing (Economics Observatory). The most substantial change in fashion production occurred in the 18th century. This was the beginning of the Industrial Revolution, and the clothing production industry was one of the first to be majorly affected by it. Inventions like the spinning jenny (1764), water frame (1769), and spinning mule (1779) allowed a single worker to spin multiple threads all at once. This drastically increased yarn production, allowing for a significant decrease in production time, as more workers could be allocated to actually creating the clothes (ThoughtCo, Misirli). These machines were quite large and complex, so with their creation came an even more centralized production, as factories of these machines became more popular, an immense increase in efficiency followed (Misirli). Power looms (1785) shortly followed this, steam powered machines, mechanizing the process of fabric creation, and although it was much more efficient labor wise, it took several decades for them to overtake hand powered looms in terms of speed (Misirli). This innovation also decreased the cost of clothes, and the persistent quality of each garment as well as making them much more accessible to the general population. But with that, it also introduced exploitive labor practices, including longer hours, unsafe working conditions, and child labor (Tuttle). While clothing availability increased, so did the human cost. This period also introduced the beginning of industrial pollution in fashion production. Textile factories required lots of water and energy, and untreated, chemical and dye filled water would be frequently discharged into nearby rivers. Although production volumes were still modest relative to modern standards, this was the first major environmental footprint of the clothing industry (Geneva Environment Network). The 19th century is where the machines we still see today come into play: the sewing machine. While early versions and designs began appearing in the very late 18th century, the more widely adopted designs emerged in the mid 1800’s. These machines drastically improved production speeds, as their quick stitching outshined any previous methods used. They were quickly adopted and easy to use, opening up the work position to people who might not have had the skills to handsew (Solinger). As a result, ready to wear clothing became even more commonplace than before, especially in urban areas. This combination of mechanized textile production and sewing machines prompted the rise of the modern day clothing factory. The process of creating a piece of clothing became even more centralized, as the factories were now producing and sewing everything in-house, rather than having to wait for the textile factory to deliver fabric to the factory where it gets turned into clothes. This shift also enabled a more standard scale for sizing, as mixups were much less probable if everything was done in the same building, along with another decrease in price (Library of Congress). Fashion began to pick up on trends, though it was nowhere near as quick as now, due to the slower forms of communication and travel. Production grew, but distribution stayed somewhat limited for the same reasons. Denim emerged during this time, and would become a key staple in the modern day for many reasons. Originally developed as a durable cotton-twill fabric in France in the 18th century, denim was designed for labor intensive work environments. Its strength was ideal for miners, railroad workers, and factory laborers. Because of its wide range of applications, this new denim material spread in popularity fast (SANVT). With the now widespread availability of cotton, the new denim material spread quickly throughout the world. This widespread popularity combined with new industrial dyeing techniques using indigo, allowed for denim to be produced efficiently and consistently. The introduction of the riveted jean by Jacob Tailor and Levi Strauss in 1873, brought a new level of quality and durability, pushing their popularity in the Americas even more (Daugherty). Entering the 20th century, the clothing industry became increasingly refined in its use of industrial systems. Sewing machines, which had already proven their efficiency in the 19th century, were now integrated into assembly line style factories, similar to already industrialized areas. Garment factories adopted task specialization, where a worker would be assigned a specific portion of the garment construction rather than sewing the whole thing themselves. This further increased production speeds, also further reducing skill threshold needed for employment, allowing for factories to hire and train workers quicker, and at a lower pay rate (Britannica, Solinger). As a result, “ready-to-wear” clothing became the dominant form of dress, replacing custom tailored everyday garments (Library of Congress). Throughout the early and mid 20th century, fashion production expanded alongside urbanization and population growth. As transportation improved, so did the distribution of clothing. With the developments of steamboats and longer railroads, finished garments could now be distributed over a much larger geographical area, increasing both availability, and in turn, demand. Fashion cycles were still slow by modern standards, trends were communicated through magazines, catalogues, and in-person retailers rather than the digital platforms we use today (Library of Congress). The second half of the 20th century marked a major turning point with the introduction and widespread adoption of synthetic fibers as substitutes for natural ones. Materials such as nylon, polyester, and acrylic offered manufacturers lower costs, increased durability, and easier care compared to natural fibers like cotton or wool. These materials were also easier to make, leading to another drop in clothing price, accelerating consumption rates, and expanding the global market (Geneva Environment Convention). Moving to the end of the 20th century, globalization had reshaped the fashion industry. Manufacturing of clothing shifted from America and Europe, to places like China, India, and Central America, where labor costs were significantly lower due to different living conditions. This is when clothing production became extremely fragmented, as raw materials might have been made on one side of the world while they were shipped and sewn together on the other. While this separation allowed for higher output, it also distanced consumers from the realities of what clothing production was becoming (Economics Observatory). Denim also followed this trajectory in the mid 1900’s. While originally being associated with durability and hard labor, its popularity shifted towards being more of a fashionable material. Youth culture began adopting it as their popularity grew through film, music and advertising. As demand increased, denim production scaled massively, relying heavily on industrial cotton farms and chemically intensive dyeing processes (Library of Congress). Indigo dyeing, once a relatively small scale operation, had grown to be this large scale production, using large volumes of water and chemicals, making denim one of the more resource-intensive fabrics to produce (Global Commons). Moving into the 21st century, fashion production reached an all time high across all categories, and became what we now call ‘fast fashion’. Advances in digital design software, automated pattern cutting machines, and computerized sewing machines allowed companies to shorten turnover times to respond more quickly to the fluctuating trends. This is where the massive issue of over consumption we see today began, as efficiency was at an all time high, so was the overproduction of these quickly passing trends. The sewing machine, though updated and automated, remained a central tool in garment construction. Alongside these developments, the environmental impact became increasingly difficult to ignore. The modern fashion industry is a large contributor to water pollution, largely due to the dyeing and finishing process, as well as being a significant source of textile waste. A majority of the synthetic fibers introduced in the 20th century are non-biodegradable and shed micro plastics during washing, contributing further to long term environmental damage. Additionally, the increased speed in consumption, has led to a fall off in the amount of care an owner puts towards their clothing, leading to premature dumping of the garment, many of which end up in landfills or are exported as waste to developing countries (Earthday). Overall, the evolution of fashion production demonstrates a clear shift from a pure necessitative, small scale craft, to highly industrialized globalized systems built around speed and volume. Clothing has evolved from a means of survival, to a trend following social construction. By the 21st century, fashion production had reached an unprecedented scale, where turnover times and efficiency began outweighing quality and sustainability, resulting in significant environmental consequences. Understanding this progression is important, as it highlights modern issues that often get overlooked.
